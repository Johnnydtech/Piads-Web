A bakery is one of the most visually compelling businesses in any neighborhood. Fresh croissants stacked on a marble counter. A towering celebration cake in the display case. A hand-piped macaron tower that took three hours to assemble. The problem is that most of that visual appeal lives inside the case — and the screens on the wall are either blank or stuck on a static menu printed six months ago.

That's a missed opportunity. The same quality and craft that goes into what you bake should show up on your screens. And when it does, it doesn't just improve the customer experience — it drives more orders, reduces end-of-day waste, and opens the door to a new revenue stream from local advertisers.

Here's how bakeries are putting their screens to work.

## Why Bakeries Are a Natural Fit for Digital Signage

Bakeries have a few built-in advantages that make digital signage particularly effective.

**Your inventory changes constantly.** What you have at 8am is different from what you have at 2pm. A paper menu can't keep up. Digital screens update in seconds — no reprinting, no crossing things out with a marker.

**Customers make impulse decisions.** People don't walk into a bakery with a strict list. They see what looks good and buy it. A screen that highlights today's special — with a photo — is the most direct path to a sale you don't have to pitch yourself.

**Dwell time is longer than it looks.** Customers wait in line, browse the case, sit with a coffee. That's 5-15 minutes of screen exposure. It's not a taproom, but it's enough to run a rotating loop of specials, seasonal items, and ads that actually get seen.

**Your customer base is exactly what local advertisers want.** Bakery regulars skew local, quality-conscious, and willing to spend on good experiences. That's a premium audience for everything from wine shops to florists to wedding venues.

## 6 Ways Bakeries Use Digital Signage

### 1. Daily Specials That Actually Stay Current

This is where digital signage pays for itself almost immediately.

Every bakery has items that come in and out of rotation — the Tuesday morning brioche, the Thursday almond croissant drop, the weekend-only cinnamon pull-apart loaf. If you're still writing specials on a chalkboard or telling customers when they reach the counter, you're missing the chance to build anticipation before they order.

Update your specials from your phone before you open each morning. By the time the first customer walks in, the screen is already doing the selling. Display:

- What's fresh today and what time it came out of the oven
- Items available in limited quantities ("Only 8 left")
- New or returning seasonal flavors
- Upcoming specials for tomorrow or the weekend (plant the seed early)

When something sells out, remove it from the screen immediately. Customers who see something on the screen and then can't get it are frustrated. Customers who never see it listed in the first place don't feel the loss.

### 2. Seasonal and Holiday Promotions

Bakeries have more natural seasonal hooks than almost any other food business. Valentine's Day heart-shaped cakes. Spring strawberry tarts. Summer fruit galettes. Fall pumpkin everything. Holiday cookie tins. Custom holiday orders.

The problem is most bakeries promote these on Instagram and hope the algorithm cooperates.

Your in-store screens reach the customers who are *already in your shop* — your highest-intent audience. They're spending money right now. A screen that shows your Mother's Day custom cake options in early May, with a clear "Order by May 7" deadline, converts at a rate no social post can match.

Schedule seasonal content weeks in advance and let it run automatically. Swap in your Valentine's Day slides in late January. Build in the Thanksgiving pie pre-order graphic in October. Your screens work ahead of the rush so you're not scrambling to update signage during your busiest weeks.

### 3. Pre-Order and Custom Order CTAs

Custom orders — wedding cakes, birthday cakes, holiday treats, corporate catering — are typically the highest-margin work a bakery does. They're also the hardest to generate without active promotion.

Your screens are a direct pipeline to customers who are already in your shop and already buying from you. Use them to plant the idea:

- "Planning a celebration? We do custom cakes and dessert tables — ask at the counter or visit piads.co/yourbakery to start a request."
- QR code leading directly to your pre-order form or contact page
- Photos of recent custom work (with permission) to show what's possible

A customer who came in for a morning pastry might walk out planning their daughter's birthday cake order. That sale doesn't happen without the prompt.

### 4. Behind-the-Counter Brand Storytelling

Artisan baking is a story product, not a commodity. The reason someone pays $6 for a croissant from your shop instead of grabbing one from a gas station is because of what you stand for — the process, the sourcing, the people behind it.

Digital screens are a great place to tell that story passively, without anyone having to say a word.

Display content like:

- Short-form video of laminating croissant dough or decorating a cake (looped, no sound needed)
- Where your flour comes from, or why you chose a specific butter
- Bios of your bakers — the person who makes the bread, the pastry chef who designed the new menu
- How long your sourdough ferments, what makes your croissants different from a chain bakery's
- Awards, press mentions, or certifications (if you have them)

This type of content builds loyalty. Customers who understand the craft become regulars. Regulars become advocates who tell other people where to go.

### 5. Upsells and Add-Ons

Most bakeries have add-ons they undersell: coffee drinks, house-made jams, specialty honey, branded merchandise, cookbook tie-ins. The counter staff is busy. They're not going to upsell on every transaction.

Your screens can do it for them.

A photo of your Earl Grey shortbread paired with a lavender latte, shown while someone waits in line, creates a mental pairing before they even reach the register. A slide for your house-made orange blossom jam — right next to the fresh croissants they're about to buy — is a simple nudge that costs nothing to run.

Think about what you'd suggest if you had 30 seconds with every customer. Put that on a slide.

### 6. Local Advertiser Revenue

This is where your screens go from a business expense to a business asset.

Your bakery customers are a genuinely desirable audience: local, food-focused, experienced buyers, and willing to pay for quality. Local businesses that want to reach exactly that person will pay to advertise in your space.

Think about who that might be:

- **Wine shops and bottle shops** — A natural pairing. Someone buying a celebration cake is probably also buying a bottle of something.
- **Florists** — Birthdays, weddings, anniversaries. The same occasions that drive bakery orders drive flower orders.
- **Wedding planners and event venues** — If you do wedding cakes, your audience and theirs overlap almost completely.
- **Coffee roasters** — Especially if you don't serve coffee yourself, a local roaster would love to reach your caffeine-and-pastry crowd.
- **Specialty food shops** — Cheese shops, olive oil stores, artisan food boutiques. Same customer, different day.
- **Catering and meal prep services** — People who value good food are interested in other services that deliver on quality.

## Where to Place Screens in a Bakery

| Location | Best Content | Why |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Above or behind the display case | Daily specials, "today's fresh items" | First thing customers look at while deciding |
| Near the register | Upsells, pre-order CTAs, seasonal promos | Last thing they see before paying |
| Waiting area / seating | Brand story, ads, seasonal content | Longer dwell time, relaxed attention |
| Front window (facing outward) | What's fresh today, hours, seasonal items | Draws foot traffic from the sidewalk |

Most bakeries do well with 1-2 screens. Start with the screen customers see while they're browsing the case — that's your highest-leverage placement.

## The Simple Shift That Changes Everything

Most bakery owners think of their TVs as something to set up and forget — a menu that lives on the wall and doesn't need to be touched.

The shift is treating your screens the same way you treat your display case: something you curate daily, with intention, to show off what's fresh and move what you need to move.

A screen that shows yesterday's specials is worse than no screen at all. A screen that shows today's fresh croissant arrival time, this weekend's cake pre-order deadline, and a QR code to book a custom order? That screen is doing real work every hour you're open.

Start with one screen in front of your display case. Update it the morning before you open. Watch what happens to the items you feature. That's the experiment — and most bakeries that run it don't go back.
